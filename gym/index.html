Phase

Training day

Warm-up — every session (8 min)

  1. Raise temp — treadmill brisk walk or cycle, easy pace · 5 min
  2. Mobility — leg swings, arm circles, hip circles, cat-cow, world's greatest stretch · 2 min
  3. Activation — 2 light "feel-out" sets of your first main exercise (50% weight) · 1 min

Cool-down — every session (5 min)

5 minutes of static stretching on the muscles you trained: hold each 20–30 seconds, breathe slowly. Lower days: quad, hamstring, hip-flexor, calf. Upper days: chest doorway, lat, shoulder cross-body, tricep overhead.

Form > ego, always For your entire first month, deliberately use weights that feel too easy. Master the movement pattern first. Injuries from rushing weight are the #1 reason beginners quit. Nobody at the gym is judging your weights — they're worried about their own.

StrategyWhy "recomposition" is your game

You're not skinny, and you're not fat — you're skinny-fat. This is the easiest starting point to mess up and one of the most rewarding to fix.

Eat
~2,000–2,150 kcal at maintenance · 120–135g protein daily
Train
4 days/week · Upper/Lower split · add weight over time
Move
8–10k steps · short walks after meals · break up sitting
Recover
7–9 hrs sleep · manage stress · one full rest day
Repeat
Stay consistent ~12 months · track waist & strength, not just scale

Skinny-fat means relatively low muscle mass with a higher-than-ideal body fat percentage, often around the belly and chest. The classic mistake is to either crash-diet (which burns your already-low muscle) or "dirty bulk" (which just adds more fat). Neither works for you.

Your strategy is body recomposition: build muscle and lose fat at the same time. Beginners with your profile can genuinely do this — untrained muscles respond dramatically ("newbie gains"), and excess body fat fuels muscle building even at maintenance calories.

The core formulaEat at roughly maintenance calories + high protein + progressive strength training + daily walking + good sleep. Be patient. Recomposition is slower on the scale but transforms how you look.

What to expect on the scale (and why not to trust it)

Your weight may barely move for months, yet your waist shrinks and your arms and shoulders fill out. This is success. The mirror, tape measure, and your strength numbers tell the real story — not the scale.

RoadmapFour phases across ~12 months

Move to the next phase only when you meet the progression criteria, not when the calendar says so. Strength benchmarks are a compass, not a pass/fail test — faster or slower is normal.

Beginner Foundation

Months 1–2 (Weeks 1–8)
Primary objectives

  • Build the habit of showing up 4×/week

  • Establish protein-focused eating without dieting
Strength benchmarks (end of phase)

  • You've trained 4×/week for at least 6 of 8 weeks
Phase 2

Early Intermediate

Months 3–5 (Weeks 9–20)
Primary objectives
  • Transition to barbell compounds (squat, deadlift, bench, OHP)
  • Apply structured progressive overload weekly
  • Increase volume gradually (more sets)
  • Visible fat loss around the waist begins
Strength benchmarks (end of phase)
  • Barbell squat: 40–50 kg × 5
  • Deadlift: 60–70 kg × 5
  • Bench press: 40–45 kg × 5
  • Overhead press: 25–30 kg × 5
Progress to Phase 3 when…
  • Compounds feel stable and you hit benchmark loads with control
  • You can add weight/reps on your own
Phase 3

Intermediate

Months 6–9 (Weeks 21–36)
Primary objectives
  • Drive real muscle growth with higher volume
  • Refine weak points (chest, rear delts, glutes for desk workers)
  • Dial in nutrition more precisely as progress slows
  • Introduce periodized deloads every 6–8 weeks
Strength benchmarks (end of phase)
  • Squat: ~1× bodyweight (60+ kg) × 5
  • Deadlift: ~1.5× bodyweight (90 kg) × 3–5
  • Bench: ~0.8× bodyweight (50 kg) × 5
  • 5+ strict pull-ups
Progress to Phase 4 when…
  • Progress naturally slows and you've built a solid base
  • You want to maintain and refine rather than chase maxes
Phase 4

Advanced Lifestyle Fitness

Month 10 onward (lifelong)
Primary objectives
  • Maintain your hard-won physique sustainably
  • Train for performance, longevity, and enjoyment
  • Auto-regulate: push hard some weeks, coast others
  • Make fitness a permanent identity, not a project
This phase has no end

Fitness woven into your life permanently. Run focused 8–12 week blocks whenever you want a specific result.

Progression & RecoveryProgressive overload, deloads, injury prevention

Progressive overload is the single principle behind all results: gradually demand more of your body over time.

The "double progression" method

  1. Pick a rep range (e.g. 10–12).
  2. Start at a weight where you can hit the bottom (10) with good form.
  3. Each session, try to add a rep. When you do the top (12) for all sets with good form…
  4. …increase weight by the smallest increment (1–2.5 kg), dropping back to ~10 reps. Repeat.
Lift typeWeight jumpHow often (beginner)
Big compounds (squat, deadlift, leg press)2.5–5 kgOften weekly early on
Upper compounds (bench, row, press)1.25–2.5 kgEvery 1–2 weeks
Isolation (curls, raises, pushdowns)1–2 kgEvery 2–3 weeks

Deloads

  • When: every 6–8 weeks, or when run-down, sleep-deprived, or lifts stall 2+ weeks.
  • How: same exercises at ~60% weight, or halve the sets. Leave feeling refreshed.
  • In Phase 1 you likely won't need formal deloads — just take an extra rest day if very sore.

Recovery

Sleep

7–9 hours. This is when muscle actually grows.

Protein timing

Spread across 4 meals. Protein within ~2 hours of training.

Rest days

Tue, Thu, Sun are growth days. Walk and stretch, let muscles repair.

Active recovery

Light walking, stretching, mobility reduces soreness.

Injury prevention

  • Never skip the warm-up.
  • Sharp pain = stop immediately. Muscle burn is fine; joint/sharp pain is not.
  • Control the lowering (eccentric) phase of every rep.
  • Leave 1–2 reps "in the tank" on most sets.
  • Prioritize face pulls and rows — they protect shoulders and fix desk posture.
  • If unsure, ask a gym trainer to watch one set.

For a skinny-fat recomp, cardio is a support tool — not the main event. Don't overdo it, or you'll burn the muscle you're building.

Structured cardio (rest or post-lifting days)

  • Beginner: 2–3×/week of 20–30 min steady, easy cardio — "Zone 2," can hold a conversation.
  • Fat loss: keep it low-intensity and consistent; after weights or on rest days, never before lifting.
  • Phase 2+ optional: 1×/week light intervals (30s faster / 90s easy × 6–8). Don't add early.
Heart healthAim for ~150 min of moderate activity per week — walking covers it. Get a basic health check (lipids, fasting glucose, HbA1c, Vitamin D, B12).
NutritionMaintenance calories, high protein

No extreme dieting. Eat at roughly maintenance calories with high protein. The magic is protein and consistency, not starvation.

LifestyleFor a desk-bound engineer

Your 8+ hours of sitting works against you. These habits matter as much as the gym.

Sleep

7–9 hours, consistent. No screens 30–60 min before bed.

Stress

Chronic stress raises cortisol → belly fat. 5–10 min daily breathing, sunlight.

Screen time

20-20-20 for eyes. A "phone down" time. Swap scrolling for a walk.

Posture

Screen at eye level, feet flat. Rows + face pulls fix "tech neck."

Weekly method

Pick one day (e.g. Sunday). Log weekly average weight, take waist measurement, confirm lifts progressing. Adjust food only if needed. 5 minutes.

Monthly review

Compare photos and measurements to last month. Waist down or stable? Lifts up? If yes — change nothing. If fat isn't moving after a month, reduce ~150 kcal. If weak and stalling, add ~150 kcal.

Phase EvolutionDoes the workout change, or just the weight?

A common beginner question: does the workout itself change each phase, or do I just add sets and weight? Both — but at different points:

  • Phases 1 → 2 (months 1–5): same workout, just heavier. You run the same Upper/Lower template the whole time. What changes is the load and volume, not the exercises — start at 2 light sets, ramp to full 3 sets, then add weight week to week. The one swap: in Phase 2 a few movements upgrade to their barbell versions (goblet squat → barbell squat, DB Romanian deadlift → barbell deadlift, DB bench → barbell bench). Same movement pattern, just the grown-up tool.
  • Phase 3 onward (month 6+): the workout itself changes. The structure changes, not just the numbers — you move to a Push / Pull / Legs / Upper split with more exercises and more sets, because by then your body adapts faster than a twice-weekly template can keep up with.

The phase selector at the top reflects exactly this — switch phases to see the load/volume notes, the Phase 2 barbell swaps, and the full Phase 3/4 Push-Pull-Legs-Upper sessions.

As you get stronger, the structure grows with you:
